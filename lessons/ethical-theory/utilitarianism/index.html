The Greatest Good

A short history of utilitarianism — the idea that the right action is the one that produces the most well-being for the most people.

One Question, One Test

Most ethical theories ask, "What kind of rule should I follow?" Utilitarianism asks something simpler:

Did the act make things better or worse?

It is a consequentialist theory: an action's morality lives entirely in its results — specifically, in how much happiness or suffering it creates. No act is forbidden in itself; what matters is the outcome.

 341–270 BCE

Epicurus & the Good Life

Long before the word "utilitarian" existed, the Greek philosopher Epicurus argued that pleasure is the only thing good in itself, and pain the only thing bad. This view is called hedonism.

But Epicurus was no party animal. The highest pleasure, he taught, was ataraxia — a calm freedom from fear and pain — reached through friendship, modest living, and clear thinking.

From a Garden to a System

Epicurus gave us the seed: well-being is what ultimately matters. But he aimed it at the individual — how should I live?

Two thousand years later, Enlightenment reformers asked a bigger version: how should we judge laws and societies? They turned hedonism into a public, almost mathematical, decision procedure.

 1748–1832

Bentham's Classical Utilitarianism

Jeremy Bentham gave the theory its famous standard: the Principle of Utility — choose whatever brings "the greatest happiness for the greatest number."

Crucially, everyone counts equally: "each to count for one, none for more than one." A king's pleasure is worth no more than a peasant's. This made utilitarianism a radical engine for reform — of prisons, laws, and animal welfare.

The Hedonic Calculus

Bentham thought you could literally add up pleasure. His "hedonic calculus" weighed pleasures by several measurable features.

Intensity — how strong the pleasure is Duration — how long it lasts Certainty — how likely it is to happen Extent — how many people it affects The moral purity of the person feeling it Whether a religious authority approves of it

Mill's Higher Pleasures

Critics mocked Bentham: if only quantity of pleasure matters, isn't a life of simple thrills as good as a life of art and thought? John Stuart Mill answered by adding quality.

Some pleasures — those of the intellect, imagination, and moral feeling — are higher than mere bodily ones. As Mill put it: "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

Act vs. Rule Utilitarianism

Act utilitarianism: judge each individual action by its own consequences. Flexible, but it can seem to justify a terrible act if it happens to maximize happiness this once.

Rule utilitarianism: follow the rules that, if generally adopted, produce the most happiness — like "don't break promises." This protects trust and rights, even when breaking the rule would help in a single case.

 20th–21st century

Modern Variants

The idea kept evolving. Preference utilitarianism (associated with Peter Singer) measures good not as raw pleasure but as satisfied preferences — getting what beings actually want.

Singer also pushed the principle of equal consideration outward to animals, and inspired effective altruism: using evidence to do the most good per dollar and effort.

Tracking the Evolution

Epicurus located the good in pleasure (hedonism) Bentham made it a public test: the greatest happiness for the greatest number Mill added that some pleasures are higher in quality Preference utilitarianism measures good as satisfied preferences Modern utilitarians abandoned the goal of maximizing well-being

The Standing Challenge

Every version faces a famous worry: by focusing only on total good, utilitarianism can seem to permit sacrificing an individual for the group — the dilemma dramatized by the trolley problem.

How a theory answers that — through rules, rights, or refinements — is exactly what keeps the 2,300-year-old conversation alive.
