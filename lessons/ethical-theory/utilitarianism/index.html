What You Did

This afternoon, on a whim, you bought a $40 slice of gold-leaf chocolate cake.

  • You ate the entire thing yourself, in about four minutes.
  • Three friends were right there. You offered them none.
  • By bite ten you felt slightly sick. By bedtime, queasy and oddly guilty.
  • Now you're asleep — and the dream is strange.

The Dream

A foggy courtroom. A giant set of scales hangs overhead. Three robed figures step forward to judge a single question:

Did that cake actually make your life better?

Each of the three is a real philosopher. Each belongs to a family of ideas called utilitarianism — the view that what's good is well-being, and the right act is the one that produces the most of it.

 341–270 BCE

The First Judge: Epicurus

"Friend," says the Greek in the laurel wreath, "I am not here to scold pleasure. I worship it."

Epicurus is a hedonist: he believes pleasure is the only thing good in itself, and pain the only thing bad in itself. Everything else — money, fame, even cake — is only good if it brings pleasure and avoids pain.

Not All Desires Are Equal

"So why," Epicurus asks, "do I frown at your cake? Because I sort desires into three bins:"

  • Natural & necessary — food when hungry, shelter, friendship, understanding. Easy to satisfy, and their absence really hurts.
  • Natural but unnecessary — a gold-leaf cake instead of plain bread. Pleasant, but it scratches an itch you didn't need to have.
  • Vain & empty — craving luxury, status, "the most expensive thing on the menu." These can never be fully satisfied, so they breed anxiety.

The Goal Is Tranquility

The aim of life, for Epicurus, isn't maximum pleasure — it's ataraxia: a calm, stable contentment, free from fear and pain.

  • Intense, fleeting thrills often cost more than they pay: your cake bought four minutes of bliss and a night of nausea and guilt. Net pain.
  • Epicurus' single greatest source of happiness? Friendship. "Of all that wisdom provides for a happy life, much the greatest is friendship."
  • And you ate alone, ignoring three friends — trading the deepest pleasure for a sugar rush.

The Second Judge: Bentham

"Pleasure, pain — yes, yes," says the man in the powdered wig. "But you forgot to do the math."

Jeremy Bentham turned hedonism into a public rule, the Principle of Utility: the right action produces "the greatest happiness for the greatest number." And in that sum, everyone counts equally — "each to count for one, none for more than one."

The Hedonic Calculus

Bentham claimed you could actually weigh a pleasure. His "hedonic calculus" scores it along several dimensions:

  • Intensity — how strong? · Duration — how long?
  • Certainty — how likely? · Propinquity — how soon?
  • Fecundity — will it lead to more pleasure? · Purity — will it avoid later pain?
  • Extent — and crucially, how many people does it reach?

Why Sharing Wins the Math

Two of those dimensions convict you, says Bentham:

  • Extent. One happy person scores far less total happiness than four happy people. Split four ways, the same cake produces more good in the world.
  • Diminishing returns. Your first bite was ecstasy; your tenth made you queasy. Pleasure per bite falls the more you eat.
  • So a bite given to a friend who's had none adds more happiness than that same bite piled onto already-stuffed you.
  • Verdict: sharing wasn't just nice — it was the option that maximized total utility.

A Voice From the Gallery

Before the third judge speaks, a bewhiskered figure — John Stuart Mill — raises a hand from the gallery. "A quick objection," he says, "about the quality of that cake's pleasure."

Mill: Higher & Lower Pleasures

Mill worried that Bentham's math treats all pleasures as the same kind of stuff. He disagreed: some pleasures are higher in quality.

  • Your cake is a lower (bodily) pleasure — fine, but shallow.
  • Conversation, art, and learning are higher pleasures, worth more even in smaller amounts.
  • As Mill put it: "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." A life of nothing but cake is the fool's bargain.

The Third Judge: Singer

A modern philosopher in glasses leans in. "Your friends aren't the only ones with a stake in that cake."

Peter Singer is a preference utilitarian: what matters is satisfying the preferences of everyone affected — and we must give equal consideration to every being capable of suffering, human or not.

Lesson One: What You Eat

Singer points at the cream and eggs. "To ignore an animal's suffering just because it isn't human is speciesism — a bias as arbitrary as racism."

  • The animals behind factory-farmed dairy and eggs have a real interest in not suffering.
  • That interest counts in the moral sum — it doesn't vanish because they can't argue in court.
  • So Singer's verdict on what to eat: lean toward foods that cause far less suffering — largely plant-based ones.

Lesson Two: That $40

"Now the price tag," says Singer, with his famous thought experiment:

  • The drowning child. If you could save a drowning child by ruining $40 shoes, you obviously should. Distance and strangers don't change the math.
  • Right now, $40 given to a highly effective charity can protect real children from deadly disease.
  • The cake bought four minutes of solo pleasure; the same money could have prevented serious suffering. This is the heart of effective altruism.

You Wake Up

Dawn. The queasiness is fading. Three lessons linger:

  • Epicurus — pleasure is the good, but tranquility and friends beat fleeting indulgence.
  • Bentham — the greatest happiness for the greatest number; sharing was the better math.
  • Singer — widen the circle: what you eat and how you spend ripple out to others, including animals.
  • Tomorrow, maybe, you buy the plain muffin — and split it three ways.
