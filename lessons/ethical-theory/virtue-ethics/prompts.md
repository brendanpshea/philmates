# Image prompts — Virtue Ethics ("The Labors of Arête")

The lesson ships with **placeholder SVGs** so it runs with zero external art.
To enhance, generate the asset below, drop the PNG in `/assets/` with the
matching name (e.g. `athena.png`), and the `src` in `index.html` already points
there. Aim for the *best* version of each: fun, gamelike, memorable, and
**informative** — every character portrait should include a visual cue that
teaches the Aristotelian concept it embodies.

The cast is **mythic Greek heroes and gods**, so full character portraits are
encouraged (no real likeness to fail at). Each portrait's design should make the
*philosophical lesson* sticky at a glance — the visual cue matters as much as
the character themselves.

## House style (prepend to every prompt)

> **16-bit SNES/JRPG pixel art**, mythic-heroic. Hard pixel edges, no
> anti-aliasing, subtle dithered shading, a chunky silhouette that reads clearly
> at 64 px. Tight palette drawn from the PhilMates UI: void background `#11131f`,
> panel slate `#1d2235`, ink `#eef1ff`, and four accent inks — go-green `#46e07a`,
> magenta `#ff6ad5`, info-blue `#4cc2ff`, amber `#ffcf5a`. Dramatic Olympian
> lighting — warm golden highlights, cool blue-purple shadows.
> **No text, no lettering, no watermark, no signature.** Transparent or `#11131f`
> background. Square (1:1); character art is a centered bust.

## Characters & scenes

### `athena.png` — Athena, goddess of wisdom (the guide)
> A commanding woman in polished bronze Corinthian armor with owl-wing motifs
> etched into the breastplate; a crested helmet pushed up to show her face — sharp
> grey eyes, a look of calm impatience. A small owl perches on her left shoulder,
> golden-eyed and alert. Her spear rests casually against one arm. Expression:
> "I'm about to teach you something important, and you'd better pay attention."
> *Teaches:* the guide/instructor — wisdom, authority, and the expectation of
> intellectual effort. The owl = wisdom; the armor = practical engagement with the
> world (not Plato's ivory tower).

### `achilles.png` — Achilles, hero of the Iliad (courage → excess)
> A powerfully built young warrior in bronze armor, mid-motion, face contorted in
> righteous fury — not monstrous but clearly past the point of reason. His
> shield is prominently **cracked** down the center, the fracture glowing faintly
> amber — the crack is the key visual: it represents the **flaw in his virtue**,
> courage broken into wrath. One hand grips a spear, the other reaches out as if
> grasping at something he's already lost. Wind whips his dark hair.
> *Teaches:* courage taken to reckless excess. The cracked shield = the doctrine
> of the mean: when virtue overshoots, it breaks. His rage after Patroclus' death
> is the Iliad's core — and Aristotle's case study for virtue gone wrong.

### `icarus.png` — Icarus, son of Daedalus (the failure of the mean)
> A young man falling through a golden sky, arms outstretched. His left wing is
> intact — white feathers, wax joints holding. His right wing is **melting**: wax
> dripping in amber droplets, feathers peeling away, the skeletal frame of the
> wing exposed. His face shows exhilaration turning to realization, not terror —
> the moment between thrill and consequence. The sun blazes above, too close.
> Below, a thin dark line of sea.
> *Teaches:* the failure of the mean made literal. Daedalus said "not too high,
> not too low" — Icarus overshot. One intact wing / one melting wing = the
> asymmetry of excess. The most iconic Greek image of what happens when you don't
> listen to wise counsel.

### `odysseus.png` — Odysseus, king of Ithaca (phronesis / practical wisdom)
> A weathered, keen-eyed man in worn leather and bronze, not posing heroically but
> *thinking* — chin resting on one fist, eyes narrowed, reading an unseen
> situation. In the background, a faint **compass rose** or **labyrinth pattern**
> rendered in blue-tinged stone or starlight — the visual metaphor for phronesis:
> navigating complex situations wisely. A coil of rope at his belt (resourceful),
> no drawn weapon (he'd rather think his way out). Stubble, sun-weathered skin,
> the look of a man who has been everywhere and learned from all of it.
> *Teaches:* phronesis = practical wisdom. Not the strongest or most passionate
> hero, but the one who *reads each situation and chooses well*. The compass/
> labyrinth = navigation through moral complexity. Contrast with Achilles' raw
> power and Medea's raw cleverness.

### `medea.png` — Medea, sorceress of Colchis (passion vs reason)
> A striking woman in dark robes edged with gold, holding a small glass vial of
> luminous green liquid at chest height. The key visual: **flames are reflected in
> her eyes** — not literal fire around her, but an inner fire visible only in
> her gaze. Her expression is the devastating one from Euripides: she *knows*
> what she's about to do, and she's doing it anyway. One hand steadies the vial;
> the other is clenched into a white-knuckled fist. Dark hair, sharp features,
> the beauty of someone dangerous.
> *Teaches:* passion overwhelming reason; cleverness without good character.
> The flames in her eyes = inner turmoil visible from outside. The vial = her
> legendary skill with poisons — she's brilliant but aimed wrong. Aristotle's
> nightmare: someone who has every intellectual gift except the one that matters
> (self-mastery / phronesis).

### `penelope.png` — Penelope, queen of Ithaca (virtue in community)
> A composed woman seated at a large wooden loom, weaving. She looks directly at
> the viewer with calm, intelligent eyes — not fragile, not waiting, but
> *managing*. The key visual: **threads radiate outward from her loom to small
> pixel-figures** at the edges of the frame — a servant, a child, a suppliant —
> representing the community her virtue sustains. The cloth on the loom has a
> subtle pattern that suggests a map or a web of connections. A small crown or
> circlet, understated — she is the queen, but her power is practical, not
> ornamental.
> *Teaches:* virtue-friendship and virtue in community. The radiating threads =
> Aristotle's point that virtue doesn't exist in isolation; it sustains and is
> sustained by relationships, obligations, and a household/polis. Twenty years of
> steadfast temperance and practical intelligence.

### `prometheus.png` — Prometheus, Titan of foresight (justice & the polis)
> A powerful figure chained to dark rock, arms stretched wide. In his right hand,
> held aloft despite the chains, **a bright flame burns** — warm golden-orange,
> the only warm color in the frame. His expression is resolved, not defeated: he
> chose this, he'd choose it again, but the cost is enormous. The chains are
> heavy, dark iron. In the far background, a faint suggestion of tiny human
> figures gathered around fires of their own — the civilization his sacrifice made
> possible. An eagle's shadow falls across one shoulder (implied, not shown — we
> know what's coming).
> *Teaches:* justice and beneficence toward humanity at enormous personal cost.
> The fire = the gift of civilization / the arts. The chains = the price. The
> distant human fires = the polis he enabled. Aristotle's hard question: is
> self-sacrifice without a supportive community truly flourishing? Can virtue
> survive without the polis?

### `olympus.png` — The Olympian training ground (scene)
> A wide establishing shot of a mythic training ground on a mountaintop above the
> clouds. Marble columns and open-air pavilions, weathered but grand; a paved
> circle at the center (an arena for discussion, not combat). Golden light spills
> from the west; the sky is deep blue-purple with early stars. No characters —
> this is the setting. A few props suggest what happens here: a scroll on a bench,
> a discarded shield, an olive branch in a bronze vase. The mood is: ancient,
> serious, beautiful — a place where you'd want to learn.
> *Teaches:* the setting — this is where the lesson takes place. Olympus as a
> place of learning, not just of gods. The discussion arena (not a battlefield)
> signals that this lesson is about thinking, not fighting.

## Real thinkers (idea-emblem relic cards)

Per the PhilMates art guidelines: **no portraits of real historical people**.
Instead, each gets an **idea-emblem relic card** — a framed 80×108 SVG with a
central icon encoding their core move. Dark border, accent inner frame, a rank
gem in the top bar, rarity pips at the bottom.

These are generated as SVGs by hand (see existing relic cards in
`lessons/ethical-theory/*/assets/`), not with the image generator. Listed here
for completeness:

- **Aristotle** — emblem: a golden balanced scale with a human figure at center
  (the mean). Accent: amber.
- **MacIntyre** — emblem: interlocking gears forming a circle (practices within
  traditions). Accent: info-blue.
- **Foot** — emblem: an oak tree with deep roots and broad canopy (natural
  goodness). Accent: go-green.
- **Hursthouse** — emblem: a compass with a human silhouette as the needle (the
  virtuous agent as guide). Accent: magenta.
