Your Job, Really

"Curing them is the easy part," the old doctor warns before retiring. "Respecting them is the trick."

  • Today you learn two skills the textbooks rush past.
  • First: how to get consent that's actually informed, not just a signature.
  • Second: how to relate to a patient — and there's more than one good way.

Before Your First Patient

Five gut-checks — no right answers. Just say where you stand; we'll revisit them at the end.

A doctor should give the facts and then step back and let the patient choose. If a patient is making a clearly bad choice, a good doctor should talk them out of it. A patient can give valid consent even without really understanding the risks. Pressuring a patient "for their own good" is sometimes acceptable. Part of a doctor's job is helping patients work out what they truly value.

Why Consent Matters

Treatment without consent — however well-meant — is something done to a patient. Consent is what makes it something done with them.

  • It's the practical face of respect for autonomy: their body, their call.
  • And it isn't one thing — valid informed consent has five ingredients.
  • Miss any one, and the "consent" is hollow.

1 · Capacity

Can this patient actually make this decision? Capacity is decision-specific — four abilities:

  • Understand the information, appreciate how it applies to them,
  • reason through the options, and communicate a choice.
  • Squirrel Nutkin grasps the words but can't appreciate the consequences — he'd cheek a hawk to its face. Capacity here is in doubt.

2 · Disclosure

Peter arrives clutching his stomach (the McGregor's-lettuce special, again). To decide, he needs the facts:

  • The diagnosis, the proposed treatment, its risks and benefits,
  • the reasonable alternatives — and what happens if he does nothing.
  • Leave out the alternatives and you've quietly made the choice for him.

How Much Must You Tell?

Two standards answer "what counts as enough disclosure?"

Disclose what a typical doctor would in your shoes.

Risk: it lets the profession decide what patients get to know.

Disclose what a typical patient would want to know to decide.

The modern standard (see Canterbury v. Spence, 1972) — it centers the patient.

3 · Understanding

Telling isn't the same as getting through. Information the patient doesn't grasp can't ground a real choice.

  • Jargon, fear, and politeness all blunt understanding — patients nod along.
  • Check it with teach-back: "So I'm sure I explained it well — what's your understanding of the plan?"
  • If Peter thinks "bed rest" means "rest in McGregor's beds," you haven't got understanding yet.

4 · Voluntariness

A choice has to be the patient's own — free of coercion, manipulation, or undue influence.

  • Jemima Puddle-Duck happily "agrees" to the sandy-whiskered gentleman's dinner plans — herb-stuffing very much included.
  • She said yes. But it was engineered, not chosen — so it isn't valid consent.
  • Watch for the foxes: flattery, half-truths, and pressure all poison voluntariness.

Influence: Fair vs Foul

Not all influence is wrong — doctors should try to persuade. The line is how.

Appeal to the patient's own reason with true, relevant arguments.

"Here's why bed rest really will get you back to mischief sooner." (Legitimate.)

Bypass reason with lies, pressure, or threats.

The fox's flattery and half-truths — consent extracted, not given.

5 · Authorization

Finally, consent is an act: the patient actually authorizes this plan.

  • Understanding and approving in your head isn't enough — they must agree to go ahead.
  • It's the patient handing you permission, not you assuming you have it.
  • (And authorization can be withdrawn — yes, even mid-lettuce-detox.)

When the Rules Bend

A few situations modify (but don't abolish) consent:

  • Emergencies — if a patient is unconscious and the need is urgent, consent is presumed.
  • Waiver — a competent patient may choose not to be informed (within limits).
  • Incapacity — when a patient genuinely can't decide, you turn to a surrogate, guided by the patient's wishes and interests.

Four Ways to Be a Doctor

Consent tells you what you must secure. But how should you relate to a patient? Ezekiel and Linda Emanuel (1992) mapped four models.

  • They differ on the doctor's role — and on what "respecting autonomy" even means.
  • Watch how each would handle the same patient.

Model 1 · Paternalistic

The doctor as guardian: you determine what's best and the patient assents.

  • Assumes shared, objective goals — mainly "get healthy."
  • Autonomy here shrinks to assenting to what the expert prescribes.
  • Fine in a true emergency; presumptuous most of the rest of the time.

Model 2 · Informative

The doctor as technician: you supply the facts; the patient supplies the values and chooses.

  • Mrs. Tiggy-Winkle wants exactly this: "Just the facts, dear — I'll decide."
  • Autonomy = control: the patient picks, you execute.
  • The risk: a doctor reduced to a vending machine, offering no guidance or care.

The Two Extremes

The doctor chooses; the patient assents.

"I know what's best for you, Peter — carrots, no garden."

The doctor informs; the patient chooses unaided.

"Here are the facts and the options. Your call entirely."

Model 3 · Interpretive

The doctor as counselor: you help the patient figure out what they already value, then choose to fit it.

  • Patients often arrive with values that are fuzzy or in conflict.
  • You help articulate them — without imposing your own.
  • Autonomy = self-understanding: choosing in line with who they really are.

Model 4 · Deliberative

The doctor as teacher and friend: you engage the patient about which health-related values are worth pursuing — and try to persuade (never coerce).

  • You don't just clarify Peter's values; you discuss whether some are better than others.
  • "A braver life needn't be a reckless one" — an argument, openly made.
  • Autonomy = moral self-development: growing through honest deliberation.

Clarify vs Develop

Help the patient uncover and apply their existing values.

"Let's name what matters most to you, and choose to match it."

Engage the patient on which values are worth adopting — persuasion, not pressure.

"May I make the case that a longer life with your sisters is worth more than the thrill?"

The Emanuels' Verdict

The Emanuels don't leave all four as equals. They argue for one as the ideal:

  • The deliberative model — physician as teacher and friend — best fits a caring relationship.
  • The paternalistic model is justified mainly in emergencies.
  • The informative model, they argue, is too thin — it abandons the patient to choose alone.
  • The aim isn't to impose values, but to care enough to deliberate about them.
