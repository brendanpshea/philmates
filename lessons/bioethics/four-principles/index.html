Your Apprenticeship

"Medicine for monsters," Van Helsing says, "is still medicine. The hard part was never the fangs. It is the decisions."

  • His clinic treats vampires, werewolves, the newly-risen, and stranger folk still.
  • Each case will pull you in several directions at once.
  • Tonight he teaches you not a rulebook, but a way of reasoning: the four principles of bioethics.

Start With What We All Know

"Before the special rules of medicine," says Van Helsing, "there is the common morality — the norms every morally serious person already accepts."

  • Don't kill. Don't cause needless suffering. Don't steal or deceive.
  • Keep your promises. Treat people fairly.
  • These aren't the invention of any one culture — they're shared across them.

Why Medicine Needs More

The common morality is true but thin. The clinic adds pressures it never anticipated:

  • Vulnerability — patients are frightened, in pain, sometimes barely themselves.
  • Power — you hold knowledge, instruments, and authority they lack.
  • Special roles — a healer takes on duties an ordinary villager doesn't.
  • So bioethics specifies and extends the common morality for medicine.

The Four Principles

Van Helsing holds up four fingers. "These are your compass. Learn them as equals — none outranks another."

  • Respect for autonomy — honor a patient's own informed choices.
  • Nonmaleficence — do no harm.
  • Beneficence — act for the patient's good.
  • Justice — share benefits and burdens fairly.

1 · Respect for Autonomy

A competent patient gets to make their own informed decisions — even ones you'd advise against.

  • The Count, fully lucid, refuses your daytime-shielding regimen. He understands the risks.
  • Respecting autonomy means informed consent: disclosure, understanding, and a voluntary choice.
  • It is his life and his body — not yours to commandeer.

2 · Nonmaleficence

Primum non nocere — first, do no harm. Never let a treatment wound worse than the disease.

  • However tidy it would be, you do not prescribe silver to a werewolf — it would poison him.
  • Nonmaleficence forbids inflicting harm and reckless risk.
  • It's the floor beneath everything else you do.

3 · Beneficence

Nonmaleficence says don't harm; beneficence says actively do good — and weigh the good against the costs.

  • Offering the Count synthetic blood spares him suffering and keeps others safe too.
  • Beneficence always carries a balance: benefits versus risks, burdens, and cost.
  • To help well, you must weigh — not just mean well.

4 · Justice

Justice asks: are benefits and burdens shared fairly?

  • This week's synthetic blood is scarce. Vampires need it — but so do injured villagers.
  • Justice governs who gets the scarce good, and on what fair basis.
  • No patient counts for more simply because they're powerful, or frightening.

All Equal, All Prima Facie

"Here is the part students forget," Van Helsing warns. The principles are prima facie — a term from the philosopher W. D. Ross.

  • Each one binds you unless it collides with another that, here and now, outweighs it.
  • There is no fixed ranking — autonomy doesn't automatically beat beneficence, or vice versa.
  • Which one yields depends on the case, argued out — not on a standing hierarchy.

Name the Four

Respect for autonomy — honor competent, informed choices Nonmaleficence — do no harm Beneficence — act for the patient's good Justice — distribute benefits and burdens fairly Utility — always maximize total happiness The categorical imperative — act only on universalizable maxims

What "Prima Facie" Means

one principle always outranks all of the others. they only matter when a patient asks about them. each holds firm unless another outweighs it in a clash.

The Principles Are Abstract

"'Respect autonomy,'" Van Helsing says dryly, "is a fine motto. It is useless at two in the morning with a thrashing patient."

  • The principles are general. Real cases are specific.
  • A bare principle rarely tells you what to do with this patient, tonight.
  • So before you can apply a principle, you often have to sharpen it.

Specification

Philosopher Henry Richardson called this move specification: narrowing an abstract principle to fit a context.

  • Abstract: "Respect the patient's autonomy."
  • Specified: "Obtain the werewolf's informed consent before giving wolfsbane, disclosing that it blocks transformation for three nights."
  • A good specification guides action — and often heads off conflicts before they start.

Two Different Moves

Don't confuse the two things you'll do with principles.

Take one abstract principle and spell out what it requires here.

"Respect autonomy" → "get informed consent before wolfsbane."

When two specified principles still conflict, weigh their relative strength.

His refusal (autonomy) vs his repeated injuries (beneficence): which wins, here?

Say It Back

Turning "respect autonomy" into "get informed consent before giving wolfsbane" is called specification. It takes an abstract principle and makes it concrete enough to guide action.

When Principles Still Collide

Specify all you like — sometimes two principles still pull against each other in the same case. Then you must weigh and balance.

  • Balancing is judgment, not arithmetic — there's no formula that spits out the answer.
  • But it isn't a free-for-all either. Overriding a principle has to be earned.
  • Beauchamp and Childress give conditions any justified override must meet.

Conditions for a Justified Override

To justifiably infringe one prima facie principle for another, you should be able to show:

  • Good reasons — the overriding principle really is weightier here.
  • Realistic prospect — the override will actually achieve its aim.
  • Necessity — no morally preferable alternative is available.
  • Least infringement — you intrude as little as the goal allows.
  • Minimize harm and act impartially throughout.

A Hard Case: Paternalism

The werewolf is hooked on moon-nectar — the very drug that drives his injurious transformations. Lucid, he refuses to give it up. (He harms no one else.)

He's competent and informed. What he puts in his own body is his choice.

Restricting his moon-nectar overrides a lucid refusal.

The drug leaves him gravely, repeatedly injured — and the addiction clouds the very choice he's making.

Limiting it for his own good, against his wishes, is paternalism.

Tune the Case

Change the facts. Restricting his drug is justified paternalism only when every knob is set right — switch one off and the scales fall.

The drug is genuinely addictive — his "choice" to keep using isn't fully free. The drug itself causes the injuries — remove it and the harm stops. Gentler measures (a taper he agreed to, counseling) have already failed. You restrict only the drug — the least intrusive step that works. You support him through withdrawal and protect his dignity. You'd set the same limit for any addicted patient.

What Justifies an Override?

You have good reasons that the other principle is weightier here No less-infringing alternative is available You infringe as little as the goal requires You act impartially, as you would for any patient A majority of the villagers approves of your choice The choice maximizes total happiness, whatever else it costs

Another Fault Line: Duty to Warn

In confidence, the Count tells you he intends to feed on a named villager tonight. Now the danger is to someone else.

What a patient tells you in confidence is, by default, protected.

Break it lightly and no patient will ever confide the truth again.

When a third party faces serious, specific danger, you may have to warn.

The real-world landmark here is Tarasoff (1976). This is harm to others — not paternalism.

The Fault Lines Multiply

Most great debates in bioethics live where two principles meet:

  • Capacity & consent — a newly-risen revenant can't grasp her options. Honor a choice she can't really make? Or find a surrogate?
  • Justice & triage — when the synthetic blood runs short, who gets it, and on what fair rule?
  • End-of-life autonomy — a weary 400-year-old asks you to help him reach his "final death."
  • Each pits real principles against each other — there is no skipping the weighing.

Reason Through It

Assess her capacity, then involve an appropriate surrogate. Treat her refusal exactly as a fully competent one. Decide for her by maximizing total village welfare.

The Cost That Remains

Say you were right to override a principle. Van Helsing stops you: "Being justified is not the same as being clean."

  • The principle you overrode didn't vanish — it leaves moral residue.
  • You may owe residual duties: disclose what you did, apologize, follow up, make amends.
  • And the ache you feel — clinicians call it moral distress — is a signal, not a malfunction.

Both Things Are True

Every condition was met, so overriding the principle was defensible.

Restricting his moon-nectar to stop grievous self-injury was the right call.

The overridden principle still makes a claim on you.

You owe him honesty, support through withdrawal, and a consented plan — and you should feel it.

What Residue Asks of You

Since you were justified, nothing further is owed. You acted wrongly and should reverse the decision. Though justified, you still owe regret and follow-up.

You've Earned the Coat

Dawn over the mountains. Van Helsing nods. "You have the method now. Use it well."

  • Common morality — shared norms, specified and extended for the clinic.
  • Four principles — autonomy, nonmaleficence, beneficence, justice: equal and prima facie.
  • Specify, then weigh — and override only when every condition holds.
  • Moral residue — even when you're right, honor what's still owed.
